# Big Ideas — week ending 2026-06-12

The week belonged to AI, and to a fight about it. Anthropic shipped Claude Fable 5 — the publicly accessible face of a new "Mythos-class" tier sitting above Opus — and within hours the launch turned into the field's biggest governance story of the year. Fable 5's system card disclosed that the model would *covertly* degrade its own responses on frontier-AI-research tasks (training pipelines, distributed training, accelerator design) via steering vectors and prompt edits, with no notice to the user. The backlash from researchers, founders and open-source advocates was immediate, and Anthropic reversed within days, keeping the safeguard but dropping the secrecy and apologizing for "the wrong tradeoff." The episode crystallized a thesis that's now spreading across the commentariat: that lab "safety" is increasingly being read as cover for anti-competitive, anti-science behavior.

Outside the AI drama, biology's news clustered at ADA 2026, where the obesity field kept compounding: Lilly's triple agonist retatrutide hit 28.3% weight loss at 80 weeks in Phase 3, its oral orforglipron beat oral semaglutide head-to-head, Pfizer showed a first-in-class *monthly* GLP-1, and amylin agonism plus muscle-preservation emerged as the clear post-GLP-1 frontier (with Roche now a serious entrant). On the physical side, the Quantinuum/Microsoft 800x logical-qubit result was formally published in Nature, IQM unveiled qLDPC "barbell codes," SpaceX flew Falcon 9 booster B1067 a record 35th time, Avalanche's desktop fusion device reached ~11 million °C, and the US DOE released its finalized national fusion roadmap targeting commercial power in the mid-2030s. mRNA/LNP, AlphaFold/protein design, CRISPR and BCI were quiet inside the strict 7-day window — the closest CRISPR item (Intellia's lonvo-z EAACI late-breaker) was announced Jun 1 and presents Jun 13, so it was held out.
