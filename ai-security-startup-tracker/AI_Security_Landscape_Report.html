Contents

1. Executive summary

This report captures a comprehensive initial survey of startups at the intersection of AI and cybersecurity. It covers 83 active companies across 12 sub-categories, summarizes notable funding rounds and exits from 2024 through Q1 2026, distills recurring themes and contrarian approaches, and proposes fifteen new-venture ideas. A companion tracker (AI_Security_Startup_Tracker.xlsx) contains the structured data and a weekly log that will be appended every Monday.

Three events have defined the 2024-2026 AI-security cycle. First, the unprecedented $32B agreement for Google to acquire Wiz in March 2025 reset the bar for platform consolidation. Second, Palo Alto Networks' acquisition of Protect AI (closed July 2025) and Cato Networks' first-ever acquisition of Aim Security (September 2025) signaled that platform vendors will buy rather than build AI-specific security. Third, Cyera's $540M Series E at a $6B valuation (June 2025) confirmed that data security for AI is the breakout category.

On the offensive side, purpose-built AI red teams and autonomous pen-testers (Haize Labs, Grey Swan, XBOW, SplxAI) are scaling alongside AI-SOC analysts (Dropzone, Prophet, Radiant, Simbian, Crogl). The agentic security layer is emerging but has not yet produced a durable category leader.

2. Methodology

Companies were selected if they (a) apply AI or ML as a first-class capability to a security problem, or (b) provide security for AI systems themselves. Pure-play AI infrastructure vendors without a security angle and pure-play security vendors without an AI angle were excluded.

Funding figures are drawn from company press releases, investor announcements, and tier-one reporting (Reuters, TechCrunch, SC World, CRN). Where the public record is thin, the tracker marks values as "n/d" (not disclosed). Valuations are post-money unless noted. The data is refreshed weekly via a scheduled task.

8. Unique approaches and differentiators

  • Reasoning-first vs. rule-first SOC: Dropzone and Prophet take an agent-reasoning approach with minimal pre-built playbooks, while Simbian and Andesite combine rule graphs with LLM reasoning. Torq and Tines extend legacy SOAR with AI-native actions.

  • Inline vs. air-gapped guardrails: Prompt Security and Lakera emphasize inline guardrails for live traffic. Protect AI (now PANW) and Cranium lean heavier on AI-BOM, scanning, and governance workflows.

  • Data-first vs. identity-first security for AI: Cyera and Sentra argue the control plane is data. Astrix, Oasis, Token, and Clutch argue it is non-human identity. Both will coexist, but consolidation into platform vendors is expected.

  • Autonomous pentest vs. continuous red team: Horizon3 and XBOW aim for full-autonomy adversaries against production targets. Haize Labs, Grey Swan, and SplxAI focus on continuous adversarial evaluation of models.

  • Detection vs. provenance for deepfakes: Reality Defender and Clarity chase better detection. GetReal (Hany Farid) bets on C2PA provenance becoming the standard. Adaptive Security wraps detection into social-engineering training and response.

  • Open-core vs. closed SaaS: Semgrep, Arize (Phoenix), Promptfoo, Socket, Endor, and Chainguard have sizable OSS footprints that drive enterprise deals. Closed SaaS incumbents like Abnormal and Material are the counter-strategy.

  • Platform absorption vs. specialist: Platform vendors (CrowdStrike, Palo Alto, Cisco, Microsoft) are bundling AI-security into existing suites. Specialists must prove that an independent category wedge survives.

9. Ideas to pursue

1. Agentic IAM / delegated authorization for AI agents

Today's IAM (Okta, Entra) was designed for humans and service accounts. AI agents need scoped, delegated, auditable capabilities per tool call, with step-up approvals and consent ceremonies. Wedge is to deeply integrate with agent frameworks (LangGraph, CrewAI, AutoGen) and offer a policy engine that reasons about capabilities, not just APIs.

2. AI-native insider risk

When employees hand work to AI copilots and agents, the copilot's actions become the attack surface. Classic IRM vendors (DTEX, Forcepoint) don't model agent context or lineage. Build an IRM that unifies user activity, copilot prompts, agent tool calls, and data lineage to score risk in real time.

3. Deepfake defense for call centers and banking ops

Voice vishing of CFOs and wire-approval desks is a weekly headline. KYC and liveness vendors fixed onboarding but not ongoing voice channels. Opportunity: a liveness + behavioral fusion layer that plugs into Genesys, NICE, Five9, and Zoom Contact Center, with an enterprise control plane.

4. Security copilot for SWE agents

Devin-class SWE agents execute shells, open PRs, and touch production systems. Enterprises will not deploy them without a guardrail layer: policy for allowed commands, code classification, PR review, and secret-scoping. Distribute as VS Code extension + GitHub App + CI plugin.

5. AI supply-chain posture (AI-BOM + provenance)

Almost no enterprise can answer 'what model, dataset, and fine-tune is in production, and how was it built.' EU AI Act Article 50 and US sector regs will force this. Build AI-BOM + provenance (C2PA) with a continuous-monitoring layer.

6. AI-native DFIR

IR playbooks remain manual; breaches like MGM, Change Healthcare, and Snowflake revealed how costly this is. Build a DFIR platform that reconstructs attacker TTPs via AI reasoning over telemetry, offers on-call AI responders, and runs as an IR-retainer SaaS with a services overlay.

7. Governed retrieval for enterprise AI

RAG pipelines routinely leak entitlements because document ACLs are stripped during vectorization. Build an ACL-aware retrieval layer and policy engine that plugs into Azure AI Search, OpenSearch, Pinecone, Weaviate, and the major Copilot surfaces.

8. Continuous AI red team as a service

Snapshot assessments are obsolete for systems whose prompts and models change weekly. Deliver continuous red teaming combining a prompt corpus, model of the app, eval harness, and attack agents. Sell into regulated industries with EU AI Act reporting obligations.

9. Small-business AI phishing defense

Abnormal and Material are enterprise-priced. With AI-phish attacking SMBs, MSPs need a defense they can bundle. Build MSP-first phishing defense with per-seat pricing, native Microsoft 365 / Google Workspace coverage, and a QR/SMS/voice layer.

10. Agent-aware secrets vending

Agents read repos and CI. Their blast radius is bigger than a human developer's. Build a secrets vending layer that issues short-lived, scoped credentials per agent invocation, with detection when an agent deviates from its declared scope.

11. EU AI Act compliance automation

GPAI model risk classification, documentation, post-market monitoring, and serious-incident reporting become mandatory in 2026. Build a platform with prebuilt taxonomies, cross-framework mappings (NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, EU AI Act), and continuous evidence generation.

12. Browser-based AI employee DLP

Employees paste secrets into ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. Network DLP misses in-browser data, and CASBs trail the long tail of AI apps. Build a managed browser + Chrome/Edge extension + LLM classifier that covers the inference channel.

13. Synthetic-media KYC and liveness

Incode, Jumio, and Onfido were built before voice/video injection attacks matured. Build sensor-fusion liveness (camera, accelerometer, microphone, active challenges) and a provenance backbone for onboarding, SIM swap, and 3DS flows.

14. AI threat-intel fusion

CTI products remain document-centric. Build an AI layer that fuses OSINT, dark-web, vuln data, and industry feeds into per-customer narratives, with agents that refine queries and produce briefs. Partner with IR firms and MSSPs for distribution.

15. Policy-as-code for AI agents

Rego/OPA was not designed for agent decision paths. Create a policy runtime that reasons about agent plans, tool calls, and data lineage, with simulation, a 'deny-explain' UX, and auditing built in.
